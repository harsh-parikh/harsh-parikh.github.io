Granular Disease Prevalence Estimation via Data Fusion

Causal framework, identification, and simulation on a synthetic map

1. Causal DAG

Drag nodes to rearrange the DAG layout.
Key structural features: R₁ is the root cause — it determines which zipcodes are available (R₁ → R₂) and directly shapes individual covariates (R₁ → X). Your zipcode in turn affects your demographics (R₂ → X). Selection S depends only on (R₁, X) — no edge from R₂ to S. Outcome Y depends on all three: R₁, R₂, and X.

2. Structural Equations

Let R₁ ∈ {1, …, J} index states and R₂ ∈ {1, …, K} index zipcodes. X ∈ ℝd denotes individual-level covariates.

State (exogenous):
  R₁ ~ P(R₁)
  Root node. Distribution over states.
Zipcode assignment:
  R₂ | R₁  ~  P(R₂ | R₁)
  Your state determines which zipcodes are available.
  e.g., P(R₂ = k | R₁ = j) ∝ Njk (population share of zip k in state j).
Individual covariates:
  X | R₁, R₂  ~  P(X | R₁, R₂)
  Your demographics are shaped by both your state and your zipcode.
  e.g., log_income | R₁, R₂ ~ N(νr₁ + τr₂,  σ²).
Outcome model:
  Y | R₁, R₂, X  ~  Bernoulli(μ(R₁, R₂, X))
  μ(r₁, r₂, x) = E[Y | R₁ = r₁, R₂ = r₂, X = x]
  e.g., μ(r₁, r₂, x) = expit(β'x + αr₁ + ζr₂)
Selection model:
  S | R₁, X  ~  Bernoulli(π(R₁, X))
  π(r₁, x) = P(S = 1 | R₁ = r₁, X = x)
  S depends only on (R₁, X). No dependence on R₂.
Zipcode membership model:
  λ(r₂ | r₁, x) = P(R₂ = r₂ | R₁ = r₁, X = x)
  Posterior probability of zipcode given state and covariates.
  By Bayes:  λ(r₂ | r₁, x) ∝ P(X = x | R₁ = r₁, R₂ = r₂) · P(R₂ = r₂ | R₁ = r₁)
  Generative direction is R₂ → X; this inverts it.
  Identified from S = 1:  P(R₂ | R₁, X, S=1) = P(R₂ | R₁, X)  (by d-separation; see below).

5. Assumptions & D-Separation Results

Assumption (Positivity):
P(S = s | R₁, X) > ε   for all s ∈ {0,1}, some ε > 0
Every (state, covariate) profile has a positive probability of appearing in either sample. This is the only assumption that must be checked empirically.
D-separation result 1 (Mean exchangeability — free from the DAG):
Y ⊥ S  |  R₁, R₂, X
Proof: Every path from S to Y goes through either R₁ or X (both conditioned on). There is no direct S → Y edge and no backdoor path. Therefore E[Y | R₁, R₂, X, S=1] = E[Y | R₁, R₂, X, S=0].
D-separation result 2 (Selection ignorability of R₂ — free from the DAG):
R₂ ⊥ S  |  R₁, X
Proof: All paths from R₂ to S:
  (i) R₂ → X → S:  blocked by conditioning on X
  (ii) R₂ ← R₁ → S:  blocked by conditioning on R₁
  (iii) R₂ ← R₁ → X → S:  blocked by conditioning on R₁ or X
Therefore P(R₂ | R₁, X, S=1) = P(R₂ | R₁, X), and λ is identifiable from S=1.

6. Identification

Step 1: Law of iterated expectations + d-separation result 1
  E[Y | R₁, R₂, S=0] = EX|R₁,R₂,S=0[ μ(R₁, R₂, X) ]

Step 2: Bayes' theorem
  P(X = x | R₁, R₂, S=0) = P(R₂ | R₁, X, S=0) · P(X | R₁, S=0) / P(R₂ | R₁, S=0)

Step 3: Apply d-separation result 2:  P(R₂ | R₁, X, S=0) = P(R₂ | R₁, X) ≡ λ(R₂ | R₁, X)
  P(X = x | R₁, R₂, S=0) = λ(R₂ | R₁, X) · f₀(X | R₁) / P(R₂ | R₁, S=0)

Step 4: Substitute into Step 1

E[Y | R₁=r₁, R₂=r₂, S=0] = EX|r₁, S=0[ μ(r₁, r₂, X) · λ(r₂ | r₁, X) ]
EX|r₁, S=0[ λ(r₂ | r₁, X) ]

All components identified:
  • μ(r₁, r₂, x) = E[Y | R₁, R₂, X, S=1] — outcome model, estimated from S=1
  • λ(r₂ | r₁, x) = P(R₂ | R₁, X) = P(R₂ | R₁, X, S=1) — zipcode membership, from S=1 (by d-sep 2)
  • f₀(x | r₁) = f(x | R₁, S=0) — covariate distribution, observed in S=0

Bayesian form of λ:
  λ(r₂ | r₁, x) = P(R₂ = r₂ | R₁ = r₁, X = x) ∝ P(X = x | R₁ = r₁, R₂ = r₂) · P(R₂ = r₂ | R₁ = r₁)
The likelihood P(X | R₁, R₂) and prior P(R₂ | R₁) are both estimable from S=1.

7. Interpretation

The identification formula says: to estimate prevalence in zipcode r₂ within state r₁, take all representative sample members in state r₁. For each such individual i, compute two quantities from models trained on the convenience sample:

(i) μ̂(r₁, r₂, Xᵢ) — the predicted outcome if this individual lived in zipcode r₂, and (ii) λ̂(r₂ | r₁, Xᵢ) — the posterior probability that this individual lives in zipcode r₂ given their covariates.

Then take the λ-weighted average of the μ predictions. Individuals whose covariate profiles are more typical of zipcode r₂ receive more weight. The denominator normalizes so the weights sum to one.

Intuitively, λ acts as a soft imputation of zipcode membership into the representative sample: rather than assigning each S=0 individual to a single zipcode, we spread them probabilistically across zipcodes according to their covariate profile, then average the outcome predictions.

1. Estimator Overview

Our goal is to estimate disease prevalence at a fine geographic resolution (R₁, R₂) — e.g., county-level — using two data sources that observe different variables. The convenience sample (S=1, e.g., EHR data from Epic Cosmos) observes everything including fine geography R₂, but suffers from selection bias. The representative sample (S=0, e.g., BRFSS survey) provides unbiased outcome data but only records coarse geography R₁.

Key Insight: The estimator combines a prediction from the biased but geographically rich sample with a reweighting over the unbiased but geographically coarse sample. The identification formula is a ratio of two expectations, both computable from observed data.

2. The Identification Formula

Under the causal DAG structure (where S depends on R₁ and X but not R₂), we derived:

E[Y | R₁=r₁, R₂=r₂, S=0]  =  EX|r₁,S=0[ μ(r₁, r₂, X) · λ(r₂ | r₁, X) ] EX|r₁,S=0[ λ(r₂ | r₁, X) ]

where the two nuisance functions are:

μ — Outcome Model

μ(r₁, r₂, x) = E[Y | R₁=r₁, R₂=r₂, X=x, S=1]

Trained on the convenience sample which observes (R₁, R₂, X, Y). Predicts the probability of disease given all variables. Valid for S=0 by d-separation: Y ⊥ S | R₁, R₂, X.

λ — Membership Model

λ(r₂ | r₁, x) = P(R₂=r₂ | R₁=r₁, X=x)

Also trained on S=1 which observes R₂. Computes the posterior probability that an individual with covariates x in state r₁ lives in county r₂. Valid for S=0 by d-separation: R₂ ⊥ S | R₁, X.

Intuition: For each individual i in the representative sample from state r₁, we compute: (a) what their predicted disease probability would be if they lived in county r₂ (that’s μ̂), and (b) how likely they actually are to live in r₂ given their demographics (that’s λ̂). The formula takes the λ-weighted average of μ predictions — individuals whose covariate profiles are more typical of county r₂ receive more weight.

3. Sample-Level Estimator

The population-level identification formula translates to a sample average. Let {(R₁i, Xi, Yi)} for i = 1, …, n₀ be the representative sample (S=0). For a target county (r₁, r₂):

θ̂(r₁, r₂)  =  Σi  μ̂(r₁, r₂, Xi) · λ̂(r₂ | r₁, Xi) · 𝟙{R₁i = r₁} Σi  λ̂(r₂ | r₁, Xi) · 𝟙{R₁i = r₁}

The indicator 𝟙{R₁i = r₁} restricts to individuals from the same state. The ratio is a Hájek-type estimator where λ̂ serves as the importance weight.

Practical Note: We only sum over S=0 individuals in the same state r₁ as the target county. This means states with fewer survey respondents will have noisier estimates. In our DGP with 500K total population, the median state has ~3,600 S=0 individuals after selection.

4. Fitting the Nuisance Models

4a. Outcome Model μ̂ — Logistic Regression

We fit a logistic regression on the convenience sample (S=1) with state dummies, county dummies, and covariates X as features:

Python # Outcome model: E[Y | R1, R2, X, S=1] from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression # Feature matrix: one-hot state + one-hot county + continuous X state_dummies = pd.get_dummies(conv['R1']) # (n_conv, n_states) county_dummies = pd.get_dummies(conv['R2']) # (n_conv, n_counties) X_covariates = conv[['X1', 'X2', 'X3']] features = np.column_stack([state_dummies, county_dummies, X_covariates]) mu_model = LogisticRegression(max_iter=500, C=0.5, solver='saga') mu_model.fit(features, conv['Y']) # Predict for county (r1, r2) given covariates X_i def predict_mu(r1, r2, X_array): n = len(X_array) s_dum = np.zeros((n, n_states)); s_dum[:, state_idx[r1]] = 1 c_dum = np.zeros((n, n_counties)); c_dum[:, county_idx[r2]] = 1 feat = np.column_stack([s_dum, c_dum, X_array]) return mu_model.predict_proba(feat)[:, 1]
Why county dummies? The county fixed effects ζr₂ capture unobserved county-level factors (e.g., food deserts, healthcare access) that affect disease prevalence beyond what X explains. Without them, we would lose the geographic signal we are trying to estimate.

4b. Membership Model λ̂ — Per-State Multinomial Logistic Regression

For each state r₁, we fit a separate multinomial logistic regression predicting county membership R₂ from covariates X. Fitted on S=1 data, valid because R₂ ⊥ S | R₁, X.

Python # Membership model: P(R2 | R1, X) per state lambda_models = {} for state in state_fips_list: state_conv = conv[conv['R1'] == state] # Need enough data and at least 2 counties represented if len(state_conv) < 20 or state_conv['R2'].nunique() < 2: continue lam = LogisticRegression(max_iter=300, C=1.0, solver='lbfgs') lam.fit( state_conv[['X1', 'X2', 'X3']].values, state_conv['R2'].values # multi-class target ) lambda_models[state] = lam
Per-state fitting: λ is fitted within each state because P(R₂ | R₁, X) only involves counties within the same state. This keeps the problem tractable — each model has at most K=10 classes (counties per state) rather than all 490.

5. Putting It All Together

The final estimation loop iterates over all counties and applies the identification formula:

Python # Identification formula: combine mu-hat and lambda-hat prevalence_estimates = {} for county_fips, info in counties.items(): state = info['state_fips'] # Get all S=0 (representative) individuals in this state rep_state = rep[rep['R1'] == state] if len(rep_state) < 10 or state not in lambda_models: prevalence_estimates[county_fips] = np.nan continue X_rep = rep_state[['X1', 'X2', 'X3']].values # Numerator and denominator of the identification formula mu_hat = predict_mu(state, county_fips, X_rep) # mu-hat(r1, r2, Xi) lam_hat = predict_lambda(state, county_fips, X_rep) # lambda-hat(r2|r1, Xi) numerator = np.sum(mu_hat * lam_hat) # Sum( mu-hat * lambda-hat ) denominator = np.sum(lam_hat) # Sum( lambda-hat ) if denominator > 0: prevalence_estimates[county_fips] = numerator / denominator else: prevalence_estimates[county_fips] = np.nan

7. Why Each Component Matters

Without μ̂ (no outcome model)

We could only use the S=0 sample mean at state level. No county-level signal at all — we would just assign the state average to every county.

Without λ̂ (no membership model)

We would average μ̂ with equal weights over all S=0 individuals in the state. This ignores that different covariate profiles are differently represented across counties — losing the “soft imputation” of geography.

Without S=0 (no representative sample)

We would have to use the S=1 covariate distribution. This is exactly the “naive” estimator — biased because selection into S=1 is correlated with the outcome via shared dependence on X.

All together

μ̂ provides the county-specific prediction, λ̂ provides the soft geography imputation, and the S=0 distribution provides unbiased covariate averaging. The combination yields a consistent estimator.

9. Bayesian Interpretation of λ

The membership model λ has a natural Bayesian interpretation:

λ(r₂ | r₁, x) = P(R₂ = r₂ | R₁ = r₁, X = x)  =  P(X = x | R₁ = r₁, R₂ = r₂)  ·  P(R₂ = r₂ | R₁ = r₁) Σk P(X = x | R₁ = r₁, R₂ = k)  ·  P(R₂ = k | R₁ = r₁)

Likelihood: P(X | R₁, R₂)

How likely are these covariates given county r₂? Counties with demographics matching individual i’s profile get higher likelihood.

Prior: P(R₂ | R₁)

Population share of county r₂ within state r₁. Larger counties have higher prior probability.

10. Connections to Existing Methods

Connection to Inverse Probability Weighting: Our estimator can be viewed as a generalized IPW where λ plays the role of the inverse propensity weight. In classical IPW, one reweights by 1/P(treatment | X). Here, λ reweights by the geographic membership probability — effectively asking “how much should this individual count toward county r₂?”
Connection to Transportability: This is a two-sample transportability problem. The convenience sample (S=1) provides a rich model of the outcome surface μ(r₁, r₂, x), but we need to “transport” these predictions to the representative population. The covariate distribution shift between S=1 and S=0 is handled by evaluating μ̂ over the S=0 covariate distribution, with λ providing the geographic refinement.

11. Doubly Robust Extension: Derivation

A natural question is whether we can make the estimator doubly robust — consistent if either the outcome model μ or a selection model π is correctly specified, rather than requiring μ alone. This requires introducing a third nuisance function:

π — Selection Propensity Model (new)

π(r₁, x) = P(S=1 | R₁=r₁, X=x)

The probability of appearing in the convenience sample given state and covariates. Estimated from the full population (both S=0 and S=1) using logistic regression with state dummies + X.

The DR estimator augments the OR estimator with a bias-correction term using S=1 residuals weighted by inverse odds of selection:

θ̂DR(r₁, r₂)  =  θ̂OR(r₁, r₂)  +  Σj∈S₁,r₁,r₂ [Yj − μ̂(r₁, r₂, Xj)] · w(Xj) Σi∈S₀,r₁ λ̂(r₂ | r₁, Xi)

where the IPW weight is the inverse odds of selection:

w(x) = 1 − π̂(r₁, x)π̂(r₁, x)
Why doubly robust? The augmentation term A(r₁,r₂) has two key properties:
• If μ̂ is correct: residuals Yj − μ̂ ≈ 0, so A ≈ 0 and θ̂DR ≈ θ̂OR. The augmentation vanishes.
• If π̂ is correct: the IPW-weighted residuals consistently estimate the bias of θ̂OR, so A corrects it.
• If both are correct: we additionally achieve the semiparametric efficiency bound.

12. DR Implementation

12a. Fitting the Selection Model π̂

Python # Selection propensity: P(S=1 | R1, X) # Fitted on the FULL population (both S=0 and S=1) # Features: state dummies + continuous X pop_state_dummies = np.zeros((len(pop), n_states)) for sf in state_fips_list: pop_state_dummies[pop['R1'] == sf, state_idx[sf]] = 1 pi_features = np.column_stack([pop_state_dummies, pop[['X1','X2','X3']]]) pi_model = LogisticRegression(max_iter=500, C=1.0, solver='saga') pi_model.fit(pi_features, pop['S']) # accuracy: 0.831

12b. Computing the DR Estimate

Python # IPW weights for S=1 individuals: w(x) = (1-pi_hat) / pi_hat pi_hat = pi_model.predict_proba(conv_features)[:, 1] ipw_weights = (1 - pi_hat) / np.clip(pi_hat, 0.05, 0.95) # trimmed # Residuals for each S=1 individual at their actual county residuals = conv['Y'] - mu_model.predict_proba(conv_features)[:, 1] for county_fips, info in counties.items(): state = info['state_fips'] # ... (compute OR estimate as before) ... # DR augmentation: S=1 individuals in this exact county in_county = (conv['R1'] == state) & (conv['R2'] == county_fips) if in_county.sum() > 0: aug = np.sum(residuals[in_county] * ipw_weights[in_county]) aug /= denominator # same denominator as OR: Sum(lambda_hat) theta_DR = theta_OR + aug else: theta_DR = theta_OR # fall back to OR when no S=1 in county
Key implementation detail: The augmentation term sums over S=1 individuals in the specific county (r₁, r₂), while the OR denominator sums over S=0 individuals in the entire state r₁. This asymmetry means the augmentation uses far fewer observations (~29 per county) than the base estimate (~3,600 per state), making it inherently noisier.

14. Why DR Underperforms: Variance–Bias Tradeoff

The DR estimator is theoretically attractive but empirically harmful in our setting. The core issue is a variance–bias tradeoff that strongly favors the simpler OR estimator:

The Bias Argument (favors DR)

DR protects against misspecification of μ̂. If the outcome model is wrong, the IPW-weighted residuals correct the bias. However, our μ̂ (logistic regression with state + county dummies + X) is well-specified for the DGP — it has OR bias of only -0.38%. There is very little bias to correct.

The Variance Argument (favors OR)

The DR augmentation sums over S=1 individuals in each specific county (~29 on average). With N=142K across 490 counties, each county’s augmentation term is based on very few observations, amplified by potentially large IPW weights. The OR estimator pools across ~3,600 S=0 individuals per state via λ-weighting — far more stable.

15. Sensitivity: Deliberately Misspecified μ̂

To give DR its best shot, we deliberately misspecified μ̂ by dropping all county dummies — removing the very geographic signal the model is supposed to capture. If DR’s bias correction works, it should recover performance:

Surprising result: Even with a deliberately misspecified outcome model, the OR estimator (RMSE=2.90%) still outperforms DR (RMSE=6.67%). The misspecified OR is actually better than the correctly specified OR (2.90% vs 3.34%) because the λ-weighting partially compensates for the missing county effects. Meanwhile, DR’s augmentation adds so much noise that it overwhelms any bias correction.

16. Conclusion: Choosing the OR Estimator

Based on the empirical comparison, we adopt the outcome regression (OR) estimator as our primary method. The DR extension, while theoretically appealing, is unsuitable for this setting because:

(1) The OR estimator already has near-zero bias (-0.38%), leaving minimal room for DR to help.

(2) The per-county sample sizes in S=1 (~29) are too small for stable IPW-weighted residuals.

(3) Even under deliberate misspecification of μ̂, DR does not recover — the variance cost dominates.

(4) The OR estimator benefits from implicit regularization through the λ-weighting, which pools information across the entire state-level S=0 sample (~3,600 individuals per state).

DR may become valuable in settings with: (a) much larger per-county S=1 samples (reducing augmentation variance), (b) outcome models that are more likely to be misspecified (e.g., nonparametric μ̂ with limited smoothing), or (c) stronger selection mechanisms where the propensity score carries more information.
